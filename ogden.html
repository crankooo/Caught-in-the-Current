Housing

Uprooted

Amid public funding chaos, post-Helene housing recovery has been slow, uneven and deeply community-driven.

By Claire Ogden

In western North Carolina, housing recovery after Tropical Storm Helene has become a story of long waits, layered bureaucracy and the neighbors, churches and nonprofits filling gaps that public systems still have not fully closed.

Kim Pierce paid $387,000 in cash for the east Asheville townhome she moved into on Monday, Sept. 23, 2024. A divorced mother of six, the 57-year-old moved to the River Knoll community to downsize, slow down from work and spend more time with her grandchildren.

But she only slept four nights in that house. Tropical Storm Helene hit on Friday, flooding Pierce's new home and destroying most of her possessions along with it. A year and a half later, she is finally settled into another townhome, this time in east Asheville's Hawthorne Villages complex about five miles west of River Knoll. She moved there in June 2025.

"It's still surreal," she said of the experience. "I mean, I couldn't wrap my brain around the loss of everything."

Pierce is one of thousands who lost housing to Helene. According to FEMA estimates cited in Buncombe County's housing needs assessment, roughly 19,951 homes were damaged by the storm, and more than 1,400 were damaged severely enough to require replacement.

But even eighteen months later, many western North Carolina residents are still not back to normal. Post-disaster housing recovery was always going to be slow, but it has faced additional roadblocks and delays amid federal funding confusion, state-administered bottlenecks and overlapping relief programs. The result is mounting frustration among people already exhausted by the cost of rebuilding, financially and emotionally.

Pierce knows this reality all too well.

"I used to be a little bit able to tell my story without crying so much," she said on a recent warm day in early spring. She is still waiting for FEMA to fully resolve claims tied to her lost home. But "it wiped me out more than anything I've ever been through, and I get frustrated that it's taken so long. I also am trying to be realistic and understanding that I'm not the only person that's in this situation."

From transitional sheltering to long recovery

For the first several weeks after Helene, government efforts were focused on disaster response and immediate shelter. At its peak in October 2024, FEMA's Transitional Sheltering Assistance program was helping 5,284 households access temporary housing, according to the Buncombe County Helene Recovery Plan. That meant hotel rooms, trailers, rental help and temporary repair support.

That emergency support has repeatedly hovered near expiration. Most recently, FEMA announced on March 16, 2026, that direct temporary housing and rental assistance for Helene survivors would be extended another six months.

Even during emergency operations, local governments were already trying to position themselves for long-term rebuilding. In January 2025, the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development allocated more than $1.4 billion to North Carolina through the Community Development Block Grant Disaster Recovery program. Because Asheville is an entitlement jurisdiction, the city also received about $225 million directly. Asheville said $31 million, or about 13%, would go toward housing recovery.

Still, many residents say churches and nonprofits have moved faster than governments. Samaritan's Purse, a Christian nonprofit headquartered in Boone, has helped replace mobile homes, build new homes and complete major repairs across the region at no cost to recipients.

Jack Bailey, a 60-year-old cook at Asheville's Hotel Indigo, is one of many who has community support to thank for both his transitional housing and his new home. Early on the Friday Helene hit, Bailey had already left his Black Mountain home for work, not knowing he would become homeless that day. He spent more than a month living at the hotel downtown and cooking for disaster response staff staying there, while his wife and child stayed with family in Atlanta and came up only occasionally for visits.

By the time Bailey finally returned home, mold had taken over after weeks without power. The house was condemned. A local church eventually gave the family a trailer, where they spent Christmas 2024 parked beside the old house. They lived in that trailer for more than a year until they could move into a new home the following December.

Despite losing the house, Bailey still owed $59,000 on the old mortgage. "Do you know anybody on this planet that would go and spend $59,000 to pay off a house that's condemned?" he said. "That's what we had to do." He and his wife paid off the mortgage on their 17th anniversary.

Bailey said FEMA did provide some money, though he has lost track of exactly how much. But it was private and nonprofit support that made the biggest difference. BeLoved Asheville gave Bailey a check for $48,000, and Samaritan's Purse later gave him $134,575.

Bailey's new house sits in the same spot as the old one, and it resembles the old place too. It was prefabricated by Clayton Homes, a Berkshire Hathaway subsidiary. It is not perfect. Walking through the house, Bailey pointed out trim that had been installed unevenly, with exposed nails where the vinyl should have covered them. It also has less storage than what the family had before.

"There's not a lot of room like we're used to," Bailey said. "But still, we're still not going to complain, because the house prices out here have gone up anyway."

He looked around a living room full of quirky furniture and secondhand finds, many from Habitat for Humanity. There was a bright fuchsia couch and a rug with a unicorn on it. "She picked it all out," he said of his wife.

Bailey is one of the lucky ones. Blue Ridge Public Radio reported in January 2026 that hundreds of people displaced by Helene were still living in RVs. And according to the City of Asheville's 2025 point-in-time count release, the number of unsheltered people counted in Buncombe County rose 50% from 2024 to 2025.

While many people remain displaced, local governments have steadily shifted from emergency response into recovery mode. By June 2025, Buncombe County had mostly transitioned out of disaster operations.

Matthew Cable, the county's community development division manager, said he served as the lead recovery support official for housing from October 2024 through January 2026. Now, he said, the work is more about tracking progress: how many applicants are moving through programs, how many homes are actually being repaired and how much unmet need remains.

In October 2025, the county hired Kevin Madsen as its first full-time recovery staffer. He now manages a small dedicated recovery team overseeing progress on the county's recovery plan, a five-year strategy that includes 114 projects across seven local governments.

The major housing programs at play, including Renew NC and the Hazard Mitigation Grant Program, are federally funded but largely state-managed. Notably, most of the biggest housing recovery streams are geared toward homeowners. Renters, in a region that was already in a housing crisis before Helene, have had fewer long-term options.

Amid all of that confusion, local governments have tried to help residents navigate what one county staffer described as the "alphabet soup" of recovery programs. Buncombe County created a Helene Resource Center to help survivors understand which applications they might qualify for and how the different aid streams fit together.

When the paperwork goes nowhere

For Nadja Simon, a personal trainer who lost her house in Swannanoa, that alphabet soup has been exhausting and mostly fruitless. Tucked away in a green house on a mountainside about ten miles east of Asheville, Simon once felt protected by the privacy and elevation of her home. Helene changed that.

Though some outsiders had called western North Carolina a "climate haven," the region's mountains also helped intensify rainfall through a process known as orographic lift, which forces air upward, cools it and wrings out additional rain. The area had already been saturated by earlier storms before Helene arrived, destabilizing slopes and priming the landscape for landslides.

Simon's house was hit by one of the more than 2,000 landslides that struck the region. Several trees fell near the house, and two separate slides sent stumps and debris barreling toward the building. Miraculously, the mass stopped just short of coming through the wall.

Simon had just installed a new sliding glass door, which she believes saved her life. "It's a miracle it didn't come inside the house," she said.

"My phone was going off, like, 'get to higher ground,'" she said. "But we are on higher ground."

Standing at that same sliding glass door eighteen months later, Simon looked out at a wall of stumps and debris still pressed against the edge of the property. She has not lived there for more than a year, but she still pays around $200 a month in electricity bills to keep mold at bay. She secured a forbearance on the mortgage for a while, but recently started paying that again, about $750 a month, on top of rent for a new apartment in south Asheville.

Since the storm, Simon has applied for nearly every form of public relief she could find, including FEMA's individual assistance program, Renew NC and the Hazard Mitigation Grant Program. Renew NC, North Carolina's single-family recovery program, is meant to help eligible homeowners repair, reconstruct or replace homes damaged by Helene. HMGP, by contrast, can fund buyouts or mitigation work for properties facing repeated or permanent risk.

Simon said Renew NC inspected her property, but she has not heard anything substantial since. She has also not heard back about the hazard mitigation application. "The frustration of not hearing anything and not knowing what's happening gets kind of wearing," she said.

At first, FEMA denied her individual assistance claim because the agency incorrectly classified her house as livable. "I lost my shit," she said. "I cried on the phone and they apologized." She eventually received about $42,000 from FEMA for damages, storage and some rental assistance, but said it took repeated follow-ups and appeals. She credits Pisgah Legal Services with helping her navigate the process.

In the meantime, Simon has had to absorb the costs of maintaining her damaged property while also paying to live somewhere else. "I don't get to walk away from that," she said.

Though the structure itself still looks intact from some angles, she said the landslides carved out a new path for water across the slope, making the site fundamentally unsafe. "There's no repairing this house," she said, "and there's no fixing this mountain."

She pointed to the underbelly of the house, where bricks had fallen away from the foundation. "The whole thing is sitting on chocolate pudding, essentially," Simon said. "So this could all just go."

Even so, Simon says she is staying. Friends, family, her synagogue and strangers have all helped keep her afloat. A colleague created a GoFundMe that raised more than $13,000. Temple Beth HaTephila provided another $31,000. FEMA eventually contributed more than $42,000. Her business, Allon Health and Wellness, survived the storm, and that has helped her hold onto something steady.

"My business is here," Simon said. "I'm rooted here. I love it here."

She paused, then described the mindset recovery has forced on her. "I've been through some things," she said, "and you know, you just put one foot in front of the other. You just keep going, right?"

Hazard mitigation, politics and the long clock

While Simon is still waiting for answers, Pierce recently learned that her application for hazard mitigation acquisition had cleared the county level and would move to the state and then FEMA.

On March 4, 2026, North Carolina Emergency Management and Buncombe County hosted a kickoff meeting for property owners whose sites had been approved to move forward in the acquisition process. According to state and county announcements, those homeowners are in the first wave of acquisitions and will now move through surveys, appraisals, purchase offers and negotiations.

Pierce is in a later group, which means her official kickoff may still be months away. She is under no illusion that the program will make her whole. Her hope, she said, is simpler than that: that it might at least allow her to pay off the mortgage on the ruined house.

"Do I get frustrated that it's taking so long?" Pierce said. Yes. But she also knows "there are far worse people off. I've tried to be thankful for what I do have."

That tension, between gratitude and exhaustion, runs through nearly every housing story in Buncombe County right now. Recovery officials describe disasters as apolitical in the sense that they affect entire communities. Yet actual recovery machinery is deeply shaped by politics, from how much scrutiny federal spending receives to how quickly state and federal agencies approve plans, reimbursements and buyouts.

Housing recovery has repeatedly run into funding delays, timeline confusion and shifting administrative expectations. For survivors, those complications often translate into more months paying mortgages on unlivable homes, more rent, more temporary fixes and more waiting.

The buyouts themselves, county officials have noted, come with their own complications. Once purchased, those properties must remain open space in perpetuity. That creates resilience benefits, but it also leaves local governments with permanent land management obligations.

Something old, something saved

Back in east Asheville, Pierce reflected on what recovery has actually looked like in practice. Some time ago, a team of volunteers from Baptist on Mission came to her River Knoll house to help remove what was left and assess whether the structure itself had been compromised. "We had to get it down to the studs," she said, "to see if structurally it had been compromised."

The engineer who came to inspect the damaged home turned out to be the same person who had inspected it before Pierce bought it. He had seen the house when it represented a fresh start. Now he was seeing what the flood had done.

"He walked in my garage and started crying," Pierce said. "There's just no words, the amount of mud, the amount of dirt, the amount of moisture" that coated everything.

Pierce tried not to get her hopes up. Her mother kept insisting that some belongings might still be salvageable.

It turned out she was right. After long hours of washing and restoration, Pierce saved a set of plates and teacups, along with three pieces of old furniture, all family heirlooms.

The most meaningful was a chandelier made from her grandmother's teacups, crafted by Pierce's mother as a Christmas gift about twenty years ago. Each cup had to be washed individually, and the chandelier had to be completely rebuilt, a restoration that cost $700. Now it hangs above her old dining room table again, just as it did before Helene.

When Pierce looks at that chandelier and thinks about everything it took to get it back, she does not see a policy program or a timeline. She sees something closer to mercy.

"It's totally grace," she said. "It doesn't feel earned or deserved, and it's a beautiful picture of people loving other people."
